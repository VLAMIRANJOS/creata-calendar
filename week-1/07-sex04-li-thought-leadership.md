When we say we are driven by "play," we are not talking about fun.

We are talking about the most powerful and least utilized force in brand activation.

Let me be specific.

In Brazil, 56.8% of consumers have purchased products motivated by memories of the past. Not because of a discount. Not because of an algorithm. Because of an emotion attached to a physical object.

83% of Brazilian consumers feel an emotional bond with items from the 80s, 90s, and 2000s. That is not a niche. That is a population.

Last week in Sao Paulo, 24 brands activated at Lollapalooza. McDonald's built a 3,000 square meter experience space. Budweiser gave attendees a mini recorder keychain to capture their own memories. Coca-Cola evolved from chaotic queues in 2025 to pre-registration via QR code in 2026.

None of these are digital campaigns. All of them are physical brand activations.

The pattern is consistent globally. The memorabilia market reached $60 billion. Nostalgia-driven campaigns generate 38% more purchase intent than traditional ones. Collectible promotions drive repeat visits at rates that digital coupons cannot match.

And yet, most brands still treat physical activation as an afterthought -- a line item in the experiential budget, not a strategic pillar.

Play is infrastructure. The kind that creates emotional equity that compounds over decades. The kind that turns a meal into a memory and a toy into a brand relationship.

We have spent 50 years building that infrastructure across 14 countries and 3 billion products. What we are seeing in the data now is not a trend. It is validation.

The brands that understand this will lead the next era of activation. The ones that do not will keep optimizing click-through rates while their competitors build lasting emotional connections.
